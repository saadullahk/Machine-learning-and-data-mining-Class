The transformed equation is $\frac{y_{1}^{2}}{\lambda _{1}}+\frac{y_{2}^{2}}{\lambda _{2}}=c$, which can be rewritten as:

$$\frac{y_{1}^{2}}{c\lambda _{1}}+\frac{y_{2}^{2}}{c\lambda _{2}}=1$$

This is the standard equation of an ellipse centered at the origin in the $(y_{1},y_{2})$ coordinate system, with semi-axes of lengths $a_{1}=\sqrt{c\lambda _{1}}$ and $a_{2}=\sqrt{c\lambda _{2}}$.

The change of variables $y=V^{T}(x-\mu )$ shows that the original coordinates $x$ are shifted by $\mu$ and then rotated by $V^{T}$. This means the ellipse is centered at $\mu $. The columns of $V$, which are the eigenvectors $v_{1}$ and $v_{2}$, correspond to the principal axes of the ellipse. Specifically, the direction of the first principal axis is $v_{1}$ and its length is $\sqrt{\lambda _{1}}$. The direction of the second principal axis is $v_{2}$ and its length is $\sqrt{c\lambda _{2}}$. The rotation angle of the ellipse's principal axes relative to the standard Cartesian axes is the angle of the eigenvectors $v_{1}$ and $v_{2}$.

d)

Let $X \sim N(\mu, \Sigma)$ and

$$D^2(X) = (X-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (X-\mu)$$

new random variable $Z=\Sigma ^{-1/2}(X-\mu )$

$$\mathbb{E}[Z]=\Sigma ^{-1/2}\mathbb{E}[X-\mu ]=\Sigma ^{-1/2}(0)=0$$

$$\text{Cov}(Z)=\mathbb{E}[ZZ^{T}]=\mathbb{E}[\Sigma ^{-1/2}(X-\mu )(X-\mu )^{T}(\Sigma ^{-1/2})^{T}]=\Sigma ^{-1/2}\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu )(X-\mu )^{T}]\Sigma ^{-1/2}=\Sigma ^{-1/2}\Sigma \Sigma ^{-1/2}=I$$

Thus, $Z\sim N(0,I)$, which means its components $Z_{1},Z_{2}$ are independent standard normal random variables.

let's express $D^{2}(X)$ in terms of $Z$.

$$(X-\mu )^{T}\Sigma ^{-1}(X-\mu )=(\Sigma ^{1/2}Z)^{T}\Sigma ^{-1}(\Sigma ^{1/2}Z)=Z^{T}(\Sigma ^{1/2})^{T}\Sigma ^{-1}\Sigma ^{1/2}Z$$

Since $\Sigma$ is symmetric, $\Sigma ^{1/2}$ is also symmetric, so $(\Sigma ^{1/2})^{T}=\Sigma ^{1/2}$.

$$D^{2}(X)=Z^{T}\Sigma ^{1/2}\Sigma ^{-1}\Sigma ^{1/2}Z=Z^{T}Z=Z_{1}^{2}+Z_{2}^{2}$$

The sum of squares of $k$ independent standard normal variables is a chi-square distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom. In this case, $k=2$, so $D^{2}(X)\sim \chi _{2}^{2}$

f)

$\textbf{K-Means:}$ algorithm for data clustering that uses the geometric distance between points to divide the data into K groups. We place each data point in the cluster where the mean is most closely located. The space is shaped like a Voronoi tessellation by the linear decision boundaries between clusters. The spherical shape and equal variance of the clusters are assumed here.

$\textbf{GMM/EM:}$ represents data as a combination of several Gaussian distributions. Each data point is associated with a posterior probability of belonging to each component. The shape of a cluster is determined by the covariance matrix $\Sigma_{k}$ of its corresponding Gaussian component. Since the equal-density contours of a Gaussian distribution form ellipses, the regions with the highest posterior probability for each component are inherently elliptical rather than spherical. This allows GMMs to flexibly capture clusters with different shapes and orientations.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def plot_clusters(X, labels, title="Clustering Result"):
    """
    X: np.array of shape (n_samples, 2)
    labels: cluster labels (list or np.array)
    """
    unique_labels = np.unique(labels)
    colors = ['r', 'b', 'y', 'g', 'c', 'm', 'k', 'orange']

    for i, label in enumerate(unique_labels):
        points = X[labels == label]
        plt.scatter(points[:, 0], points[:, 1],
                    c=colors[i % len(colors)],
                    label=f"Cluster {label}", s=10)

    plt.xlabel("Dimension 1")
    plt.ylabel("Dimension 2")
    plt.title(title)
    plt.legend()
    plt.show()
